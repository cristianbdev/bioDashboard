## Design Context

### Users

- **Admin / Researchers**: Authors of the biosecurity assessment tool. They use the dashboard to monitor, analyze, and manage evaluation results across multiple aquaculture facilities. Need dense data, filtering, comparative views, and user management.
- **Producers / Facility Managers**: Farm owners who have been evaluated. They check their own facility's results. Not necessarily technical — they need to understand what the scores mean and what actions to take.
- **Public / Visitors**: General access to aggregate or public-facing data. Needs to be immediately understandable without domain expertise.

**Context**: Users may be viewing this in offices, on-site at aquaculture facilities, or on mobile devices in the field. Lighting conditions vary, and internet may not be perfect.

**Job to be done**: Understand biosecurity performance at a glance, identify risks, and know what to improve.

### Brand Personality

- **Calm**: Even when risks are high, the interface communicates clearly without alarm. Red signals are factual, not panic-inducing.
- **Trustworthy**: Data is presented with precision and scientific rigor. Numbers use a mono font. Sources and methodology are accessible.
- **Approachable**: Despite being a scientific tool, it never feels cold or intimidating. Explanations, tooltips, and plain language bridge the gap between researchers and producers.

**3-word summary**: Calm, trustworthy, precise.

**Emotional goal**: Build confidence in the data and the next steps, regardless of the user's technical background.

### Aesthetic Direction

- **Visual tone**: A hybrid of modern SaaS polish and scientific clarity. Think "Vercel meets research dashboard" — clean, refined, but data-first.
- **Theme**: Light mode as default (reflects the clean, airy feel of aquaculture environments). Dark mode fully supported for low-light use and personal preference.
- **Color anchor**: Teal (`#0f766e`) is the brand color and must remain central. It evokes water, biology, and trust. Accent with blues, ambers, and reds only for semantic meaning (info, warning, danger).
- **Typography**: Geist for UI text (clean, modern), IBM Plex Mono for numeric/scientific data (precision, tabular alignment).
- **References**: 
  - Scientific side: Grafana, Tableau — information density, clear hierarchy.
  - Modern side: Linear, Vercel — whitespace, rounded corners, subtle shadows, purposeful motion.
- **Anti-references**: 
  - Avoid overly playful or cartoonish styles (this is a professional monitoring tool).
  - Avoid purely clinical / unstyled data dumps (producers need guidance, not raw tables).
  - Avoid high-contrast neon or aggressive color palettes.

### Design Principles

1. **Clarity over density** — Every chart, metric, and label must answer "what does this mean?" at a glance. Use tooltips, plain-language subtitles, and contextual badges liberally.
2. **Risk is signal, not noise** — Risk states (high/medium/low) must be instantly scannable through color and iconography, but never feel alarming or punitive. Red means "attention needed," not "disaster."
3. **Responsive by default, mobile by necessity** — Producers and admins may check this on tablets or phones in the field. Every layout, chart, and interaction must work gracefully down to small screens.
4. **Scientific precision, human explanation** — Numbers and charts are accurate and use scientific formatting (mono font, proper units), but every section should explain what it means and why it matters.
5. **Consistency across roles** — The visual language (colors, spacing, components, card-flat style) must be identical across admin, producer, and public views. Only the content density and feature set change.
6. **Accessibility is non-negotiable** — Color is never the sole indicator of state. All interactive elements have clear focus states. Dark mode is not an afterthought.
